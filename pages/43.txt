					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Integration of software development and operations</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>DevOps</b> is the integration and automation of the <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Information technology operations">information technology operations</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>a<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>. DevOps encompasses necessary tasks of <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> and can lead to shortening development time and improving the <a href="/wiki/Systems_development_life_cycle" title="Systems development life cycle">development life cycle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> According to Neal Ford, DevOps, particularly through <a href="/wiki/Continuous_delivery" title="Continuous delivery">continuous delivery</a>, employs the "Bring the pain forward" principle, tackling tough tasks early, fostering automation and swift issue detection.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Software_programmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Software programmer">Software programmers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_architect" title="Software architect">architects</a> should use <a href="/wiki/Fitness_function" title="Fitness function">fitness function</a> to keep their software in check.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Although debated,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>b<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>c<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>d<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>e<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> DevOps is characterized by key principles: shared ownership, workflow automation, and rapid feedback.
From an academic perspective, <a href="/wiki/Len_Bass" title="Len Bass">Len Bass</a>, Ingo Weber, and Liming Zhu—three computer science researchers from the <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Software_Engineering_Institute" title="Software Engineering Institute">Software Engineering Institute</a>—suggested defining DevOps as "a set of practices intended to reduce the time between committing a change to a system and the change being placed into normal production, while ensuring high quality".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
However, the term is used in multiple contexts. At its most successful, DevOps is a combination of specific practices, culture change, and tools.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Proposals to combine software development methodologies with deployment and operations concepts began to appear in the late 80s and early 90s.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, the first conference named DevOps Days was held in <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, Belgium. The conference was founded by Belgian consultant, project manager and agile practitioner Patrick Debois.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The conference has now spread to other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, a report called "State of DevOps" was first published by Alanna Brown at <a href="/wiki/Puppet_(software)" title="Puppet (software)">Puppet Labs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2016_State_of_DevOps_Report_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2016_State_of_DevOps_Report-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2014, the annual State of DevOps report was published by <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Forsgren" title="Nicole Forsgren">Nicole Forsgren</a>, Gene Kim, Jez Humble and others. They stated that the adoption of DevOps was accelerating.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Also in 2014, Lisa Crispin and Janet Gregory wrote the book More Agile Testing, containing a chapter on testing and DevOps.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the <a href="/wiki/DevOps_Research_and_Assessment" title="DevOps Research and Assessment">DORA</a> metrics for throughput (deployment frequency, lead time for changes), and stability (mean time to recover, change failure rate) were published in the State of DevOps report.<sup id="cite_ref-2016_State_of_DevOps_Report_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2016_State_of_DevOps_Report-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the research methodology and metrics were criticized by experts.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In response to these criticisms, the 2023 State of DevOps report <sup id="cite_ref-2023_State_of_DevOps_Report_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2023_State_of_DevOps_Report-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> published changes that updated the stability metric "mean time to recover" to "failed deployment recovery time" acknowledging the confusion the former metric has caused.<sup id="cite_ref-Culture_is_everything_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Culture_is_everything-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Relevant_metrics">Relevant metrics</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relevant metrics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/DevOps_Research_and_Assessment" title="DevOps Research and Assessment">DevOps Research and Assessment</a> (DORA) has developed a series of metrics which are intended to measure software development efficiency and reliability. These metrics include: <sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Deployment Frequency: Time between code deployments.</li>
<li>Mean Lead Time for Changes: Time between code commit and deployment.</li>
<li>Change Failure Rate: Percentage of deployments causing production issues.</li>
<li>Mean Time To Recovery: Time to resolve production issues.</li>
<li>Reliability (added in 2021): Measures operational performance, focusing on availability and adherence to user expectations.</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Relationship_to_other_approaches">Relationship to other approaches</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship to other approaches"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Many of the ideas fundamental to DevOps practices are inspired by, or mirror, other well known practices such as <a href="/wiki/Lean_manufacturing" title="Lean manufacturing">Lean</a> and <a href="/wiki/W._Edwards_Deming" title="W. Edwards Deming">Deming's</a> <a href="/wiki/PDCA" title="PDCA">Plan-Do-Check-Act</a> cycle, through to <a href="/wiki/The_Toyota_Way" title="The Toyota Way">The Toyota Way</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">Agile</a> approach of breaking down components and batch sizes.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Contrary to the "top-down" prescriptive approach and rigid framework of <a href="/wiki/ITIL" title="ITIL">ITIL</a> in the 1990s, DevOps is "bottom-up" and flexible, having been created by software engineers for their own needs.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Agile">Agile</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Agile"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">Agile software development</a></div>
<p>The motivations for what has become modern DevOps and several standard DevOps practices such as automated build and test, <a href="/wiki/Continuous_integration" title="Continuous integration">continuous integration</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Continuous_delivery" title="Continuous delivery">continuous delivery</a> originated in the Agile world, which dates (informally) to the 1990s, and formally to 2001. Agile development teams using methods such as <a href="/wiki/Extreme_programming" title="Extreme programming">extreme programming</a> couldn't "satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software"<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> unless they took responsibility for operations and infrastructure for their applications, automating much of that work. Because <a href="/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)" title="Scrum (software development)">Scrum</a> emerged as the dominant Agile framework in the early 2000s and it omitted the engineering practices that were part of many Agile teams, the movement to automate operations and infrastructure functions splintered from Agile and expanded into what has become modern DevOps. Today, DevOps focuses on the deployment of developed software, whether it is developed using Agile oriented methodologies or other methodologies.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="ArchOps">ArchOps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: ArchOps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>ArchOps presents an extension for DevOps practice, starting from <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">software architecture</a> artifacts, instead of source code, for operation deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> ArchOps states that architectural models are first-class entities in software development, deployment, and operations.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Continuous_Integration_and_Delivery_(CI/CD)"><span id="Continuous_Integration_and_Delivery_.28CI.2FCD.29"></span>Continuous Integration and Delivery (CI/CD)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Continuous Integration and Delivery (CI/CD)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/CI/CD" title="CI/CD">CI/CD</a></div>
<p>Automation is a core principle for achieving DevOps success and CI/CD is a critical component.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Plus, improved collaboration and communication between and within teams helps achieve faster <a href="/wiki/Time_to_market" title="Time to market">time to market</a>, with reduced risks.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Mobile_DevOps">Mobile DevOps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mobile DevOps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mobile_DevOps" title="Mobile DevOps">Mobile DevOps</a></div>
<p>Mobile DevOps is a set of practices that applies the principles of DevOps specifically to the development of mobile applications. Traditional DevOps focuses on streamlining the <a href="/wiki/Software_development_process" title="Software development process">software development process</a> in general, but <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app_development" title="Mobile app development">mobile development</a> has its own unique challenges that require a tailored approach.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Mobile DevOps is not simply as a branch of DevOps specific to mobile app development, instead an extension and reinterpretation of the DevOps philosophy due to very specific requirements of the mobile world.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Site-reliability_engineering">Site-reliability engineering</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Site-reliability engineering"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Site_reliability_engineering" title="Site reliability engineering">Site reliability engineering</a></div>
<p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Site_reliability_engineering" title="Site reliability engineering">site reliability engineering</a> (SRE), an approach for releasing new features continuously into large-scale high-availability systems while maintaining high-quality end-user experience.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While SRE predates the development of DevOps, they are generally viewed as being related to each other. Some of the original authors of the discipline consider SRE as an implementation of DevOps.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Toyota_production_system,_lean_thinking,_kaizen"><span id="Toyota_production_system.2C_lean_thinking.2C_kaizen"></span>Toyota production system, lean thinking, kaizen</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toyota production system, lean thinking, kaizen"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Production_System" title="Toyota Production System">Toyota Production System</a></div>
<p>Toyota production system, also known under the acronym TPS, was the inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Lean_thinking" title="Lean thinking">lean thinking</a> with its focus on <a href="/wiki/Continuous_improvement_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous improvement process">continuous improvement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaizen" title="Kaizen">kaizen</a>, flow and small batches. The <a href="/wiki/Andon_(manufacturing)" title="Andon (manufacturing)">andon cord principle</a> to create fast feedback, swarm and solve problems stems from TPS.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="DevSecOps,_shifting_security_left"><span id="DevSecOps.2C_shifting_security_left"></span>DevSecOps, shifting security left</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: DevSecOps, shifting security left"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><i>DevSecOps</i> is an augmentation of DevOps to allow for security practices to be integrated into the DevOps approach. Contrary to a traditional centralized security team model, each delivery team is empowered to factor in the correct security controls into their software delivery. Security practices and testing are performed earlier in the development lifecycle, hence the term "<a href="/wiki/Shift-left_testing" title="Shift-left testing">shift left</a>". Security is tested in three main areas: static, software composition, and dynamic.
</p><p>Checking software statically via <a href="/wiki/Static_application_security_testing" title="Static application security testing">static application security testing</a> (SAST) is <a href="/wiki/White-box_testing" title="White-box testing">white-box testing</a> with special focus on security. Depending on the programming language, different tools are needed to do such static code analysis. The software composition is analyzed, especially libraries, and the version of each component is checked against vulnerability lists published by <a href="/wiki/Computer_emergency_response_team" title="Computer emergency response team">CERT</a> and other expert groups. When giving software to clients, library licenses and their match to the license of the software distributed are in focus, especially <a href="/wiki/Copyleft" title="Copyleft">copyleft</a> licenses.
</p><p>In dynamic testing, also called <a href="/wiki/Black-box_testing" title="Black-box testing">black-box testing</a>, software is tested without knowing its inner functions. In DevSecOps this practice may be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_application_security_testing" title="Dynamic application security testing">dynamic application security testing</a> (DAST) or penetration testing. The goal is early detection of defects including <a href="/wiki/Cross-site_scripting" title="Cross-site scripting">cross-site scripting</a> and <a href="/wiki/SQL_injection" title="SQL injection">SQL injection</a> vulnerabilities. Threat types are published by the <a href="/wiki/OWASP" title="OWASP">open web application security project</a>, e.g. its TOP10,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and by other bodies. 
</p><p>DevSecOps has also been described as a cultural shift involving a holistic approach to producing secure software by integrating security education, security by design, and security automation.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Culture">Culture</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>DevOps initiatives can change how a company's <a href="/wiki/Information_technology_operations" class="mw-redirect" title="Information technology operations">operations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Software_developer" class="mw-redirect" title="Software developer">developers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">testers</a> collaborate during the development and delivery processes.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>DevOps attempts to supports consistency, reliability, and efficiency within an organization. This is usually enabled by a shared code repository or version control.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Many organizations use <a href="/wiki/Version_control" title="Version control">version control</a> to facilitate DevOps automation technologies like <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machines" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual machines">virtual machines</a>, containerization (or <a href="/wiki/OS-level_virtualization" title="OS-level virtualization">OS-level virtualization</a>), and <a href="/wiki/CI/CD" title="CI/CD">CI/CD</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Git" title="Git">Git</a> version control system and the <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a> platform referenced as examples.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="GitOps">GitOps</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: GitOps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>GitOps evolved from DevOps. The specific state of deployment configuration is <a href="/wiki/Version-control" class="mw-redirect" title="Version-control">version-controlled</a>. Because the most popular <a href="/wiki/Version-control" class="mw-redirect" title="Version-control">version-control</a> is <a href="/wiki/Git" title="Git">Git</a>, GitOps' approach has been named after <a href="/wiki/Git" title="Git">Git</a>. Changes to configuration can be managed using <a href="/wiki/Code_review" title="Code review">code review</a> practices, and can be rolled back using version-controlling. Essentially, all of the changes to a code are tracked, bookmarked, and making any updates to the history can be made easier. As explained by <a href="/wiki/Red_Hat" title="Red Hat">Red Hat</a>, <i>"visibility to change means the ability to trace and reproduce issues quickly, improving overall security."</i><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Best_practices_for_cloud_systems">Best practices for cloud systems</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DevOps&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Best practices for cloud systems"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The following practices can enhance productivity of DevOps <a href="/wiki/CI/CD" title="CI/CD">pipelines</a>, especially in systems hosted in the <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud</a>: <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Number of Pipelines</b>: Small teams can be more productive by having one <a href="/wiki/Repository_(version_control)" title="Repository (version control)">repository</a> and one pipeline. In contrast, larger organizations may have separate repositories and pipelines for each team or even separate repositories and pipelines for each service within a team.</li>
<li><b>Permissions</b>: In the context of <a href="/wiki/CI/CD" title="CI/CD">pipeline-related</a> permissions, adhering to the <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_least_privilege" title="Principle of least privilege">principle of least privilege</a> can be challenging due to the dynamic nature of <a href="/wiki/Software_architecture" title="Software architecture">architecture</a>. Administrators may opt for more permissive permissions while implementing compensating <a href="/wiki/Security_controls" title="Security controls">security controls</a> to minimize the blast radius.</li></ul>
