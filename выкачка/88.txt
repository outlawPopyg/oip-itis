<p><b>Integration testing</b>, also called <b>integration and testing</b>, abbreviated <b>I&amp;T</b>, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">software testing</a> in which multiple parts of a <a href="/wiki/Software_system" title="Software system">software system</a> are tested as a group. 
</p><p>Integration testing describes tests that are run at the integration-level to contrast testing at the <a href="/wiki/Unit_testing" title="Unit testing">unit</a> or <a href="/wiki/System_testing" title="System testing">system</a> level.
</p><p>Often, integration testing is conducted to evaluate the <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_compliance" title="Regulatory compliance">compliance</a> of a component with <a href="/wiki/Functional_requirement" title="Functional requirement">functional requirements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>In a structured development process, integration testing takes as its input <a href="/wiki/Module_(programming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Module (programming)">modules</a> that have been unit tested, groups them in larger aggregates, applies tests defined in an integration <a href="/wiki/Test_plan" title="Test plan">test plan</a>, and delivers as output test results as a step leading to system testing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Approach">Approach</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Integration_testing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Approach"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some different types of integration testing are big-bang, mixed (sandwich), risky-hardest, <a href="/wiki/Top-down_and_bottom-up_design" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-down and bottom-up design">top-down, and bottom-up</a>. Other Integration Patterns<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> are: collaboration integration, backbone integration, layer integration, client-server integration, distributed services integration and high-frequency integration.
</p><p>In big-bang testing, most of the developed modules are coupled together to form a complete software system or major part of the system and then used for integration testing. This method is very effective for saving time in the integration testing process <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. However, if the test cases and their results are not recorded properly, the entire integration process will be more complicated and may prevent the testing team from achieving the goal of integration testing.
</p><p>In bottom-up testing, the lowest level components are tested first, and are then used to facilitate the testing of higher level components. The process is repeated until the component at the top of the hierarchy is tested. All the bottom or low-level modules, procedures or functions are integrated and then tested. After the integration testing of lower level integrated modules, the next level of modules will be formed and can be used for integration testing. This approach is helpful only when all or most of the modules of the same development level are ready. This method also helps to determine the levels of software developed and makes it easier to report testing progress in the form of a percentage.
</p><p>In top-down testing, the top integrated modules are tested first and the branch of the module is tested step by step until the end of the related module.
</p><p>Sandwich testing combines top-down testing with bottom up testing. One limitation to this sort of testing is that any conditions not stated in specified integration tests, outside of the confirmation of the execution of design items, will generally not be tested.
</p>
