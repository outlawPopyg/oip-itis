					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Collection of loosely coupled services used to build computer applications</div>
<p>In software engineering, a <b>microservice</b> architecture is an architectural pattern that organizes an application into a collection of loosely coupled, fine-grained services that communicate through lightweight protocols. This pattern is characterized by the ability to develop and deploy services independently, improving modularity, scalability, and adaptability. However, it introduces additional complexity, particularly in managing distributed systems and inter-service communication, making the initial implementation more challenging compared to a monolithic architecture. <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Definition">Definition</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There is no single, universally agreed-upon definition of microservices. However, they are generally characterized by a focus on modularity, with each service designed around a specific business capability. These services are loosely coupled, independently deployable, and often developed and scaled separately, enabling greater flexibility and agility in managing complex systems. Microservices architecture is closely associated with principles such as domain-driven design, decentralization of data and governance, and the flexibility to use different technologies for individual services to best meet their requirements. <sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NMMA16_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMMA16-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Usage">Usage</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>It is common for microservices architectures to be adopted for <a href="/wiki/Native_cloud_application" title="Native cloud application">cloud-native applications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serverless_computing" title="Serverless computing">serverless computing</a>, and applications using lightweight <a href="/wiki/Operating-system-level_virtualization" class="mw-redirect" title="Operating-system-level virtualization">container</a> deployment. According to Fowler, because of the large number (when compared to monolithic application implementations) of services, decentralized continuous delivery and <a href="/wiki/DevOps" title="DevOps">DevOps</a> with holistic service monitoring are necessary to effectively develop, maintain, and operate such applications.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A consequence of (and rationale for) following this approach is that the individual microservices can be individually scaled. In the monolithic approach, an application supporting three functions would have to be scaled in its entirety even if only one of these functions had a resource constraint.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> With microservices, only the microservice supporting the function with resource constraints needs to be scaled out, thus providing resource and cost optimization benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2020, the Cloud Microservices Market Research Report predicted that the global microservice architecture market size will increase at a <a href="/wiki/Compound_annual_growth_rate" title="Compound annual growth rate">CAGR</a> of 21.37% from 2019 to 2026 and reach $3.1 billion by 2026.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Microservices_and_AI_Integration">Microservices and AI Integration</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Microservices and AI Integration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into microservices architecture enhances scalability and flexibility in application development. Deploying AI models as independent microservices allows for modularity and continuous improvement without overhauling entire systems. This approach supports rapid innovation cycles essential for AI development. <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><b>Applications of AI in Microservices:</b>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Dynamic Load Balancing:</b> AI models optimize workload distribution, improving service performance.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Predictive Analytics:</b> AI-based microservices analyze historical data and forecast demand, enhancing system reliability.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Autonomous Services:</b> AI-driven microservices power chatbots, fraud detection, and recommendation engines.</li></ul>
<p>Challenges include managing distributed AI models, ensuring real-time inference, and securing API-driven AI interactions.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Cell-Based_Architecture_in_Microservices">Cell-Based Architecture in Microservices</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cell-Based Architecture in Microservices"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Cell-based architecture is a <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a> design in which computational resources are organized into self-contained units called cells. Each cell operates independently, handling a subset of requests while maintaining scalability, fault isolation, and availability. <sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A cell typically consists of multiple microservices and functions as an autonomous unit. In some implementations, entire sets of microservices are replicated across multiple cells, enabling requests to be rerouted to another operational cell if one experiences a failure. This approach is intended to improve system-wide resilience by limiting the impact of localized failures. <sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Some implementations incorporate <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker_design_pattern" title="Circuit breaker design pattern">circuit breakers</a> within and between cells. Within a cell, circuit breakers may be used to mitigate cascading failures among microservices, while inter-cell circuit breakers can isolate failing cells and redirect traffic to those that remain operational. <sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Cell-based architecture has been adopted in certain large-scale distributed systems where fault isolation and redundancy are design priorities. Its implementation varies based on system requirements, infrastructure constraints, and specific operational goals. <sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In 1999, software developer Peter Rodgers had been working on the Dexter research project at <a href="/wiki/Hewlett_Packard_Labs" title="Hewlett Packard Labs">Hewlett Packard Labs</a>, whose aim was to make code less brittle and to make large-scale, complex software systems <a href="/wiki/Robustness_(computer_science)" title="Robustness (computer science)">robust</a> to change.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Ultimately this path of research led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Resource-oriented_computing" title="Resource-oriented computing">resource-oriented computing</a> (ROC), a generalized computation abstraction in which REST is a special subset. In 2005, during a presentation at the Web Services Edge conference, Rodgers argued for "<a href="/wiki/Representational_state_transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Representational state transfer">REST</a>-services" and stated that "<a href="/wiki/Software_component" title="Software component">Software components</a> are Micro-Web-Services... Micro-Services are composed using <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_(Unix)" title="Pipeline (Unix)">Unix-like pipelines</a> (the Web meets Unix = true <a href="/wiki/Loose_coupling" title="Loose coupling">loose-coupling</a>). Services can call services (+multiple language run-times). Complex service assemblies are abstracted behind simple <a href="/wiki/URI" class="mw-redirect" title="URI">URI</a> interfaces. Any service, at any granularity, can be exposed." He described how a well-designed microservices platform "applies the underlying architectural principles of the <a href="/wiki/WWW" class="mw-redirect" title="WWW">Web</a> and REST services together with Unix-like scheduling and pipelines to provide radical flexibility and improved simplicity in service-oriented architectures.<sup id="cite_ref-cloudcomputingexpo_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloudcomputingexpo-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2005, <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Cockburn" title="Alistair Cockburn">Alistair Cockburn</a> wrote about <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_architecture_(software)" title="Hexagonal architecture (software)">hexagonal architecture</a> which is a software design pattern that is used along with the microservices. This pattern makes the design of the microservice possible since it isolates in layers the business logic from the auxiliary services needed in order to deploy and run the microservice completely independent from others.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Microservice_granularity">Microservice granularity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microservice granularity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Determining the appropriate level of (micro)service granularity in a microservices architecture often requires iterative collaboration between architects and developers. This process involves evaluating user requirements, service responsibilities, and architectural characteristics, such as non-functional requirements. Neal Ford highlights the role of integrator and disintegrator factors in this context. Integrator factors, such as shared transactions or tightly coupled processes, favor combining services, while disintegrator factors, such as fault tolerance or independent scalability, encourage splitting services to meet operational and architectural goals. Additionally, fitness functions, as proposed by Neal Ford, can be used to validate architectural decisions and service granularity by continuously measuring system qualities or behaviors that are critical to stakeholders, ensuring alignment with overall architectural objectives. <sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mapping_Microservices_to_Bounded_Contexts">Mapping Microservices to Bounded Contexts</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mapping Microservices to Bounded Contexts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A bounded context, a fundamental concept in Domain-Driven Design (DDD), defines a specific area within which a domain model is consistent and valid, ensuring clarity and separation of concerns. <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In microservices architecture, a bounded context often maps to a microservice, but this relationship can vary depending on the design approach. A one-to-one relationship, where each bounded context is implemented as a single microservice, is typically ideal as it maintains clear boundaries, reduces coupling, and enables independent deployment and scaling. However, other mappings may also be appropriate: a one-to-many relationship can arise when a bounded context is divided into multiple microservices to address varying scalability or other operational needs, while a many-to-one relationship may consolidate multiple bounded contexts into a single microservice for simplicity or to minimize operational overhead. The choice of relationship should balance the principles of DDD with the system's business goals, technical constraints, and operational requirements. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Benefits">Benefits</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Benefits"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The benefit of decomposing an application into different smaller services are numerous:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Modular_programming" title="Modular programming">Modularity</a>: This makes the application easier to understand, develop, test, and become more resilient to architecture erosion.<sup id="cite_ref-Micro_Chen_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Micro_Chen-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This benefit is often argued in comparison to the complexity of monolithic architectures.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Scalability" title="Scalability">Scalability</a>: Since microservices are implemented and deployed independently of each other, i.e. they run within independent processes, they can be monitored and scaled independently.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/System_integration" title="System integration">Integration</a> of heterogeneous and <a href="/wiki/Legacy_system" title="Legacy system">legacy systems</a>: microservices are considered a viable means for modernizing existing monolithic software application.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  There are experience reports of several companies who have successfully replaced parts of their existing software with microservices or are in the process of doing so.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The process for <a href="/wiki/Software_modernization" title="Software modernization">software modernization</a> of legacy applications is done using an incremental approach.<sup id="cite_ref-researchgate.net_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-researchgate.net-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Distributed development: it parallelizes <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">development</a> by enabling small autonomous teams to develop, <a href="/wiki/Software_deployment" title="Software deployment">deploy</a> and scale their respective services independently.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It also allows the architecture of an individual service to emerge through continuous <a href="/wiki/Refactoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Refactoring">refactoring</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ach_Chen_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ach_Chen-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Microservice-based architectures facilitate <a href="/wiki/Continuous_integration" title="Continuous integration">continuous integration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Continuous_delivery" title="Continuous delivery">continuous delivery</a> and deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Criticism_and_concerns">Criticism and concerns</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism and concerns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The microservices approach is subject to criticism for a number of issues:
</p>
<ul><li>Services form information barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-infoq-1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infoq-1-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Inter-service calls over a network have a higher cost in terms of network latency and message processing time than in-process <a href="/wiki/Function_call" class="mw-redirect" title="Function call">calls</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_system" title="Monolithic system">monolithic</a> service process.<sup id="cite_ref-martinfowler_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martinfowler-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Software_testing" title="Software testing">Testing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Software_deployment" title="Software deployment">deployment</a> can be complicated.<sup id="cite_ref-cdct_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdct-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Moving responsibilities between services is more difficult.<sup id="cite_ref-Micro_Chen_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Micro_Chen-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It may involve communication between different teams, rewriting the functionality in another language or fitting it into a different infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-martinfowler_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-martinfowler-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, microservices can be deployed independently from the rest of the application, while teams working on monoliths need to synchronize to deploy together.<sup id="cite_ref-researchgate.net_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-researchgate.net-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Viewing the size of services as the primary structuring mechanism can lead to too many services when the alternative of internal modularization may lead to a simpler design. This requires understanding the overall architecture of the applications and interdependencies between components.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Two-phase_commit_protocol" title="Two-phase commit protocol">Two-phased commits</a> are regarded as an anti-pattern in microservices-based architectures, resulting in a tighter coupling of all the participants within the transaction. However, the lack of this technology causes awkward dances which have to be implemented by all the transaction participants in order to maintain data consistency.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Development and support of many services are more challenging if they are built with different tools and technologies - this is especially a problem if engineers move between projects frequently.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The protocol typically used with microservices (HTTP) was designed for public-facing services, and as such is unsuitable for working internal microservices that often must be impeccably reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-Löwy_2019_73–75_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Löwy_2019_73–75-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>While not specific to microservices, the decomposition methodology often uses functional decomposition, which does not handle changes in the requirements while still adding the complexity of services.<sup id="cite_ref-Löwy_2019_73–75_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Löwy_2019_73–75-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The very concept of microservice is misleading since there are only services. There is no sound definition of when a service starts or stops being a microservice.<sup id="cite_ref-Löwy_2019_73–75_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Löwy_2019_73–75-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Data aggregation. In order to have a full view of a working system, it is required to extract data sets from the microservices repositories and aggregate them into a single schema. For example, to be able to create operational reports that are not possible using a single microservice repository.</li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Complexities">Complexities</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Complexities"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The architecture introduces additional complexity and new problems to deal with, such as <a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">latency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Message_format" title="Message format">message format</a> design,<sup id="cite_ref-ieeeswmsip2_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ieeeswmsip2-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Backup" title="Backup">backup</a>/availability/consistency (BAC),<sup id="cite_ref-ieeeccbac_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ieeeccbac-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Load_balancing_(computing)" title="Load balancing (computing)">load balancing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fault_tolerance" title="Fault tolerance">fault tolerance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-infoqbook_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infoqbook-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> All of these problems have to be addressed at scale. The complexity of a <a href="/wiki/Monolithic_application" title="Monolithic application">monolithic application</a> does not disappear if it is re-implemented as a set of microservices. Some of the complexity gets translated into operational complexity.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other places where the complexity manifests itself are increased network traffic and resulting in slower performance. Also, an application made up of any number of microservices has a larger number of interface points to access its respective <a href="/wiki/Software_ecosystem" title="Software ecosystem">ecosystem</a>, which increases the architectural complexity.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Various organizing principles (such as <a href="/wiki/Hypermedia_as_the_Engine_of_Application_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypermedia as the Engine of Application State">hypermedia as the engine of application state</a> (HATEOAS), interface and data model documentation captured via <a href="/wiki/Swagger_(software)" title="Swagger (software)">Swagger</a>, etc.) have been applied to reduce the impact of such additional complexity.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Deployment_Strategies">Deployment Strategies</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Deployment Strategies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Microservices are deployed using various methodologies, including serverless computing and edge computing, which reduce infrastructure overhead while enhancing scalability.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Serverless_and_Edge_Computing">Serverless and Edge Computing</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Serverless and Edge Computing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Microservices are increasingly deployed using serverless computing and edge computing, reducing infrastructure overhead and enhancing scalability.
</p><p><b>Serverless Microservices:</b> Serverless computing allows microservices to run on-demand without managing servers. Platforms such as AWS Lambda, Azure Functions, and Google Cloud Functions automatically allocate resources based on execution needs.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  
Advantages include:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Auto-scaling</b> – Services automatically adjust to workload changes.</li>
<li><b>Reduced Costs</b> – No charges for idle infrastructure, only active execution time.</li>
<li><b>Simplified Maintenance</b> – Eliminates the need for manual provisioning.</li></ul>
<p><b>Edge Computing with Microservices:</b> Deploying microservices at the network edge enables real-time data processing for IoT, 5G, and AI-powered applications.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Instead of central cloud servers, microservices run on edge devices, reducing latency and bandwidth costs.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Autonomous Vehicles</b> – AI microservices process sensor data locally for real-time decision-making.</li>
<li><b>Smart Cities</b> – Microservices enable intelligent traffic management and real-time monitoring.</li>
<li><b>Content Delivery Networks (CDN)</b> – Caches microservices closer to users for faster data retrieval.</li></ul>
<p>Challenges include cold start latency in serverless deployments and security risks in edge networks.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Antipatterns">Antipatterns</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Antipatterns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li>The "Data-Driven Migration AntiPattern", coined by Mark Richards, highlights the challenges of prioritizing data migration during the transition from a monolithic to a microservices architecture. To address this antipattern, an iterative approach can be helpful where application code is migrated first, with new microservices temporarily relying on the existing monolithic database. Over time, as the system is better understood, data can be decoupled and restructured, enabling individual microservices to operate with their own databases. This strategy can simplify the migration process and reduce data migration errors. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The "Timeout AntiPattern," coined by Mark Richards, describes the challenges of setting timeout values in distributed systems. Short timeouts may fail legitimate requests prematurely, leading to complex workarounds, while long timeouts can result in slow error responses and poor user experiences. The <a href="/wiki/Circuit_breaker_design_pattern" title="Circuit breaker design pattern">Circuit Breaker Pattern</a> can address these issues by monitoring service health through mechanisms such as heartbeats, "synthetic transactions", or real-time usage monitoring. This approach can enable faster failure detection and can improve the overall user experience in distributed architectures. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Reporting on microservices data presents challenges, as retrieving data for a reporting service can either break the <a href="/wiki/Domain-driven_design" title="Domain-driven design">bounded contexts</a> of microservices, reduce the timeliness of the data, or both. This applies regardless of whether data is pulled directly from databases, retrieved via HTTP, or collected in batches. Mark Richards refers to this as the "<i>Reach-in Reporting Antipattern"</i>. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A possible alternative to this approach is for databases to asynchronously push the necessary data to the reporting service instead of the reporting service pulling it. While this method requires a separate contract between microservices and the reporting service and can be complex to implement, it helps preserve bounded contexts while maintaining a high level of data timeliness. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Challenges">Challenges</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Challenges"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Microservices are susceptible to the <a href="/wiki/Fallacies_of_distributed_computing" title="Fallacies of distributed computing">fallacies of distributed computing</a>, a series of misconceptions that can lead to significant issues in software development and deployment. <sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Code_Sharing_Challenges">Code Sharing Challenges</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Code Sharing Challenges"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Ideally, microservices follow a "share-nothing" architecture. However, in practice, microservices architectures often encounter situations where code must be shared across services. Common approaches to addressing this challenge include utilizing separate shared libraries for reusable components (e.g., a security library), replicating stable modules with minimal changes across services, or, in certain cases, consolidating multiple microservices into a single service to reduce complexity. Each approach has its advantages and trade-offs, depending on the specific context and requirements. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_46-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Best_practices">Best practices</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Best practices"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>According to O'Reilly, each microservice should have its own architectural characteristics (a.k.a <a href="/wiki/Non-functional_requirement" title="Non-functional requirement">non functional requirements</a>), and architects should not define uniform characteristics for the entire <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Technologies">Technologies</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Technologies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Computer microservices can be implemented in different programming languages and might use different infrastructures. Therefore, the most important technology choices are the way microservices communicate with each other (synchronous, asynchronous, UI integration) and the protocols used for the communication (RESTful HTTP, messaging, <a href="/wiki/GraphQL" title="GraphQL">GraphQL</a> ...). In a traditional system, most technology choices like the programming language impact the whole system. Therefore, the approach to choosing technologies is quite different.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_Foundation" title="Eclipse Foundation">Eclipse Foundation</a> has published a specification for developing microservices, Eclipse MicroProfile.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Service_mesh">Service mesh</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microservices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Service mesh"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1236090951">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Service_mesh" title="Service mesh">Service mesh</a></div>
<p>In a service mesh, each service instance is paired with an instance of a reverse proxy server, called a service proxy, sidecar proxy, or sidecar. The service instance and sidecar proxy share a container, and the containers are managed by a container orchestration tool such as <a href="/wiki/Kubernetes" title="Kubernetes">Kubernetes</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomad_(software)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nomad (software) (page does not exist)">Nomad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Docker_(software)" title="Docker (software)">Docker Swarm</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Apache_Mesos" title="Apache Mesos">DC/OS</a>. The service proxies are responsible for communication with other service instances and can support capabilities such as service (instance) discovery, load balancing, authentication and authorization, secure communications, and others.
</p>
