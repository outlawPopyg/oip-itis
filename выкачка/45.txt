<p><b>Scrum</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Agile_management" title="Agile management">agile</a> team collaboration framework commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Software_development" title="Software development">software development</a> and other industries. 
</p><p>Scrum prescribes for teams to break work into <a href="/wiki/Goal" title="Goal">goals</a> to be completed within <a href="/wiki/Timeboxing" title="Timeboxing">time-boxed</a> iterations, called <i>sprints</i>. Each sprint is no longer than one month and commonly lasts two weeks. The scrum team assesses progress in time-boxed, <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_meeting" title="Stand-up meeting">stand-up meetings</a> of up to 15 minutes, called <i>daily scrums</i>. At the end of the sprint, the team holds two further meetings: one sprint review to demonstrate the work for <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a> and solicit feedback, and one internal <a href="/wiki/Retrospective#Software_development" title="Retrospective">sprint retrospective</a>. A person in charge of a scrum team is typically called a <b>scrum master</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Scrum's approach to product development involves bringing <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a> authority to an operational level.<sup id="cite_ref-schwaber_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Unlike a sequential approach to product development, scrum is an <a href="/wiki/Iterative_design" title="Iterative design">iterative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iterative_and_incremental_development" title="Iterative and incremental development">incremental</a> framework for product development.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumalliance_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumalliance-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Scrum allows for continuous feedback and flexibility, requiring teams to self-organize by encouraging physical <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">co-location</a> or close online collaboration, and mandating frequent communication among all team members. The flexible approach of scrum is based in part on the notion of requirement volatility, that stakeholders will change their requirements as the project evolves.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The use of the term <i>scrum</i> in software development came from a 1986 <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i> paper titled "The New New Product Development Game" by <a href="/wiki/Hirotaka_Takeuchi" title="Hirotaka Takeuchi">Hirotaka Takeuchi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ikujiro_Nonaka" title="Ikujiro Nonaka">Ikujiro Nonaka</a>. Based on case studies from manufacturing firms in the automotive, photocopier, and printer <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector">industries</a>, the authors outlined a new approach to product development for increased speed and flexibility. They called this the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a> approach, as the process involves a single <a href="/wiki/Cross-functional_team" title="Cross-functional team">cross-functional team</a> operating across multiple overlapping phases in which the team "tries to go the distance as a unit, passing the ball back and forth".<sup id="cite_ref-TakeuchiNonaka_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TakeuchiNonaka-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The authors later developed scrum in their book, <i>The Knowledge Creating Company</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>In the early 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Schwaber" title="Ken Schwaber">Ken Schwaber</a> used what would become scrum at his company, Advanced Development Methods. <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Sutherland" title="Jeff Sutherland">Jeff Sutherland</a>, John Scumniotales, and Jeff McKenna developed a similar approach at Easel Corporation, referring to the approach with the term <i>scrum</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Sutherland and Schwaber later worked together to integrate their ideas into a single framework, formally known as scrum. Schwaber and Sutherland tested scrum and continually improved it, leading to the publication of a research paper in 1995,<sup id="cite_ref-OOPSLA95_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OOPSLA95-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Manifesto_for_Agile_Software_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Manifesto for Agile Software Development">Manifesto for Agile Software Development</a> in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-agilemanifesto_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agilemanifesto-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Schwaber also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Babatunde_Ogunnaike" title="Babatunde Ogunnaike">Babatunde Ogunnaike</a> at DuPont Research Station and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Delaware" title="University of Delaware">University of Delaware</a> to develop Scrum. Ogunnaike believed that software development projects could often fail when initial conditions change if <a href="/wiki/Product_management" title="Product management">product management</a> was not rooted in empirical practice.<sup id="cite_ref-schwaber_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>In 2002, Schwaber with others founded the Scrum Alliance and set up the <i>Certified Scrum</i> accreditation series.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Schwaber left the Scrum Alliance in late 2009 and subsequently founded Scrum.org, which oversees the parallel <i>Professional Scrum</i> accreditation series.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Since 2009, a public document called <i>The Scrum Guide</i><sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidesite_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidesite-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> has been published and updated by Schwaber and Sutherland. It has been revised six times, with the most recent version having been published in November 2020.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Scrum_team">Scrum team</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scrum team"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A scrum team is organized into at least three categories of individuals: the product owner, developers, and the scrum master. The product owner liaises with stakeholders, those who have an interest in the project's outcome, to communicate tasks and expectations with developers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Developers in a scrum team organize work by themselves, with the facilitation of a scrum master.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Product_owner">Product owner</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Product owner"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Each scrum team has one product owner.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The product owner focuses on the business side of product development and spends the majority of time liaising with stakeholders and the team. The role is intended to primarily represent the product's <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_the_customer" title="Voice of the customer">voice of the customer</a>, or the desires of a <a href="/wiki/Committee" title="Committee">committee</a>, and bears responsibility for the delivery of business results.<sup id="cite_ref-Essential_Scrum:_Velocity_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Essential_Scrum:_Velocity-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Product owners manage the <a href="/wiki/Product_backlog" title="Product backlog">product backlog</a> and are responsible for maximizing the value that a team delivers.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They do not dictate the technical solutions of a team but may instead attempt to seek consensus among team members.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidepdf2016_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidepdf2016-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>As the primary liaison of the scrum team towards stakeholders, product owners are responsible for communicating announcements, project definitions and progress, RIDAs (<a href="/wiki/Project_risk_management" title="Project risk management">risks</a>, impediments, <a href="/wiki/Dependency_(project_management)" title="Dependency (project management)">dependencies</a>, and assumptions), funding and scheduling changes, the product backlog, and project governance, among other responsibilities.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="The current source is insufficiently reliable (WP:NOTRS). (July 2023)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Product owners can also cancel a sprint if necessary, without the input of team members.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidesite_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidesite-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Developers">Developers</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Developers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In scrum, the term <i>developer</i> or <i>team member</i> refers to anyone who plays a role in the development and support of the product and can include researchers, <a href="/wiki/Systems_architect" title="Systems architect">architects</a>, designers, programmers, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidesite_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidesite-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Scrum_master">Scrum master</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scrum master"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Scrum is facilitated by a scrum master, whose role is to educate and coach teams about scrum theory and practice.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidepdf2020_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidepdf2020-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Scrum masters have differing roles and responsibilities from traditional <a href="/wiki/Team_leader" title="Team leader">team leads</a> or <a href="/wiki/Project_manager" title="Project manager">project managers</a>. Some scrum master responsibilities include coaching, objective setting, problem solving, oversight, planning, backlog management, and communication facilitation.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidepdf2020_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidepdf2020-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On the other hand, traditional project managers often have <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">people management</a> responsibilities, which a scrum master does not. Scrum teams do not involve project managers, so as to maximize self-organisation among developers.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumprimer_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumprimer-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Workflow">Workflow</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Workflow"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Sprint">Sprint</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sprint"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Hackathon#Code_sprints" title="Hackathon">Hackathon §&#160;Code sprints</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Scrum_Framework.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Scrum_Framework.png/220px-Scrum_Framework.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Scrum_Framework.png/330px-Scrum_Framework.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Scrum_Framework.png/440px-Scrum_Framework.png 2x" data-file-width="1189" data-file-height="662" /></a><figcaption>The scrum framework (PBI in the figure refers to product backlog item)</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Scrum_process.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Scrum_process.svg/220px-Scrum_process.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Scrum_process.svg/330px-Scrum_process.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Scrum_process.svg/440px-Scrum_process.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="500" /></a><figcaption>The scrum process</figcaption></figure>
<p>A sprint (also known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Design_sprint" title="Design sprint">design sprint</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Iterative_and_incremental_development" title="Iterative and incremental development">iteration</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Timeboxing" title="Timeboxing">timebox</a></i>) is a fixed period of time wherein team members work on a specific goal. Each sprint is normally between one week and one month, with two weeks being the most common.<sup id="cite_ref-schwaber_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The outcome of the sprint is a functional deliverable, or a product which has received some development in <a href="/wiki/Iterative_and_incremental_development" title="Iterative and incremental development">increments</a>. When a sprint is abnormally terminated, the next step is to conduct new sprint planning, where the reason for the termination is reviewed. 
</p><p>Each sprint starts with a sprint planning event in which a sprint goal is defined. Priorities for planned sprints are chosen out of the backlog. Each sprint ends with two events:<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A <i>sprint review</i> (progress shown to stakeholders to elicit their feedback)</li>
<li>A <i>sprint retrospective</i> (identifying lessons and improvements for the next sprints)</li></ul>
<p>The suggested maximum duration of sprint planning is eight hours for a four-week sprint.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidesite_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidesite-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Daily_scrum">Daily scrum</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Daily scrum"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Daily_scrum_meeting" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily scrum meeting">Daily scrum meeting</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/220px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/330px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg/440px-Daily_sprint_meeting.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1936" /></a><figcaption>A daily scrum in the computing room</figcaption></figure>
<p>Each day during a sprint, the developers hold a daily scrum (often conducted <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_meeting" title="Stand-up meeting">standing up</a>) with specific guidelines, and which may be facilitated by a scrum master.<sup id="cite_ref-schwaber_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Daily scrum meetings are intended to be less than 15 minutes in length, taking place at the same time and location daily. The purpose of the meeting is to announce progress made towards the sprint goal and issues that may be hindering the goal, without going into any detailed discussion. Once over, individual members can go into a 'breakout session' or an 'after party' for extended discussion and collaboration.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Scrum masters are responsible for ensuring that team members use daily scrums effectively or, if team members are unable to use them, providing alternatives to achieve similar outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Post-sprint_events">Post-sprint events</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Post-sprint events"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Conducted at the end of a sprint, a sprint review is a meeting that has a team share the work they've completed with stakeholders and liaise with them on feedback, expectations, and upcoming plans. At a sprint review completed deliverables are demonstrated to stakeholders. The recommended duration for a sprint review is one hour per week of sprint.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidesite_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidesite-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A sprint retrospective is a separate meeting that allows team members to internally analyze the strengths and weaknesses of the sprint, future areas of improvement, and <a href="/wiki/Continual_improvement_process" title="Continual improvement process">continuous process improvement</a> actions.<sup id="cite_ref-Essential_Scrum:_Velocity2_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Essential_Scrum:_Velocity2-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Backlog_refinement">Backlog refinement</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Backlog refinement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Backlog refinement is a process by which team members revise and prioritize a backlog for future sprints.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEProject_Management_Institute2021Glossary_§3_Definitions_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEProject_Management_Institute2021Glossary_§3_Definitions-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It can be done as a separate stage done before the beginning of a new sprint or as a continuous process that team members work on by themselves. Backlog refinement can include the breaking down of large tasks into smaller and clearer ones, the clarification of success criteria, and the revision of changing priorities and returns.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Artifacts">Artifacts</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Artifacts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Artifact_(software_development)" title="Artifact (software development)">Artifacts</a> are a means by which scrum teams manage product development by documenting work done towards the project. There are seven scrum artifacts, with three of them being the most common: product backlog, sprint backlog, and increment.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Product_backlog">Product backlog</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Product backlog"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Product_backlog" title="Product backlog">Product backlog</a></div>
<p>The product backlog is a breakdown of work to be done and contains an ordered list of <a href="/wiki/Requirement" title="Requirement">product requirements</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Software_feature" title="Software feature">features</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patch_(computing)" title="Patch (computing)">bug fixes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-functional_requirement" title="Non-functional requirement">non-functional requirements</a>) that the team maintains for a <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">product</a>. The order of a product backlog corresponds to the urgency of the task. Common formats for backlog items include <a href="/wiki/User_story" title="User story">user stories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Use_case" title="Use case">use cases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumprimer_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumprimer-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The product backlog may also contain the product owner's assessment of <a href="/wiki/Business_value" title="Business value">business value</a> and the team's assessment of the product's effort or complexity, which can be stated in <a href="/wiki/Planning_poker" title="Planning poker">story points</a> using the rounded <a href="/wiki/Fibonacci_sequence" title="Fibonacci sequence">Fibonacci scale</a>. These estimates try to help the product owner gauge the timeline and may influence the ordering of product backlog items.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p><p>The product owner maintains and prioritizes product backlog items based on considerations such as risk, business value, dependencies, size, and timing. High-priority items at the top of the backlog are broken down into more detail for developers to work on, while tasks further down the backlog may be more vague.<sup id="cite_ref-schwaber_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwaber-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Sprint_backlog">Sprint backlog</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sprint backlog"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The sprint backlog is the subset of items from the product backlog intended for developers to address in a particular sprint.<sup id="cite_ref-MartinelliMilosevic2016_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MartinelliMilosevic2016-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Developers fill this backlog with tasks they deem appropriate to fill the sprint, using past performance to assess their capacity for each sprint. The scrum approach has tasks on the sprint backlog not assigned to developers by any particular individual or leader. Team members self organize by pulling work as needed according to the backlog priority and their own capabilities and capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumguidepdf2017_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumguidepdf2017-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Increment">Increment</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Increment"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>An increment is a potentially releasable output of a sprint, which meets the sprint goal. It is formed from all the completed sprint backlog items, integrated with the work of all previous sprints.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Other_artifacts">Other artifacts</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Other artifacts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Burndown_chart">Burndown chart</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Burndown chart"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:SampleBurndownChart.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SampleBurndownChart.svg/385px-SampleBurndownChart.svg.png" decoding="async" width="385" height="192" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SampleBurndownChart.svg/578px-SampleBurndownChart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/SampleBurndownChart.svg/770px-SampleBurndownChart.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="867" data-file-height="433" /></a><figcaption>A sample burndown chart for a completed sprint, showing remaining effort at the end of each day</figcaption></figure>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Burndown_chart" title="Burndown chart">Burndown chart</a></div>
<p>Often used in scrum, a burndown chart is a publicly displayed chart showing remaining work.<sup id="cite_ref-Cobb2015_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cobb2015-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It provides quick visualizations for reference. The horizontal axis of the burndown chart shows the days remaining, while the vertical axis shows the amount of work remaining each day. During sprint planning, the ideal burndown chart is plotted. Then, during the sprint, developers update the chart with the remaining work.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Release_burnup_chart">Release burnup chart</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Release burnup chart"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:SampleBurnupChart.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/384px-SampleBurnupChart.png" decoding="async" width="384" height="246" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/576px-SampleBurnupChart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/SampleBurnupChart.png/768px-SampleBurnupChart.png 2x" data-file-width="986" data-file-height="632" /></a><figcaption>A sample burnup chart for a release, showing scope completed each sprint (MVP = <a href="/wiki/Minimum_viable_product" title="Minimum viable product">minimum viable product</a>)</figcaption></figure>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Burnup_chart" class="mw-redirect" title="Burnup chart">Burnup chart</a></div>
<p>Updated at the end of each sprint, the release burn-up chart shows progress towards delivering a forecast scope. The horizontal axis of the release burnup chart shows the sprints in a release, while the vertical axis shows the amount of work completed at the end of each sprint. 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Velocity">Velocity</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Velocity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Velocity_(software_development)" title="Velocity (software development)">Velocity (software development)</a></div>
<p>Some project managers believe that a team's total capability effort for a single sprint can be derived by evaluating work completed in the last sprint. The collection of historical "<a href="/wiki/Velocity_(software_development)" title="Velocity (software development)">velocity</a>" data is a guideline for assisting the team in understanding their capacity.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Limitations">Limitations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Limitations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some have argued that scrum events, such as daily scrums and scrum reviews, hurt <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a> and waste time that could be better spent on actual productive tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Scrum has also been observed to pose difficulties for part-time or geographically distant teams; those that have highly specialized members who would be better off working by themselves or in working cliques; and those that are unsuitable for incremental and <a href="/wiki/Development_testing" title="Development testing">development testing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Adaptations">Adaptations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Adaptations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Scrum is frequently tailored or adapted in different contexts to achieve varying aims.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A common approach to adapting scrum is the combination of scrum with other software development methodologies, as scrum does not cover the whole <a href="/wiki/Software_lifecycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Software lifecycle">product development lifecycle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Various scrum practitioners have also suggested more detailed techniques for how to apply or adapt scrum to particular problems or organizations. Many refer to these techniques as 'patterns', an analogous use to <a href="/wiki/Design_pattern" title="Design pattern">design patterns</a> in architecture and software.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Scrumban">Scrumban</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Scrumban"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1236090951" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scrumban" title="Scrumban">Scrumban</a></div>
<p>Scrumban is a software production model based on scrum and <a href="/wiki/Kanban_(development)" title="Kanban (development)">kanban</a>. To illustrate each stage of work, teams working in the same space often use post-it notes or a large whiteboard.<sup id="cite_ref-scrumban_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumban-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Kanban models allow a team to visualize work stages and limitations.<sup id="cite_ref-knibergskarin_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knibergskarin-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Scrum_of_scrums">Scrum of scrums</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Scrum of scrums"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Scrum of scrums is a technique to operate scrum at scale for multiple teams coordinating on the same product. Scrum-of-scrums daily scrum meetings involve ambassadors selected from each individual team, who may be either a developer or scrum master. As a tool for coordination, scrum of scrums allows teams to collectively work on team-wide risks, impediments, dependencies, and assumptions (RIDAs), which may be tracked in a backlog of their own.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scrumofscrums_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scrumofscrums-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Large-scale_scrum">Large-scale scrum</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Large-scale scrum"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Large-scale scrum is an organizational system for product development that scales scrum with varied rules and guidelines, developed by Bas Vodde and <a href="/wiki/Craig_Larman" title="Craig Larman">Craig Larman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>50<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There are two levels to the framework: the first level, designed for up to eight teams; and the second level, known as 'LeSS Huge', which can accommodate development involving hundreds of developers.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>51<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Criticism">Criticism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Criticism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1251242444" /><table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></span></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Scrum_(software_development)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2024</span>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A systematic review found "that Distributed Scrum has no impact, positive or negative on overall project success" in distributed software development.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>52<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Fowler_(software_engineer)" title="Martin Fowler (software engineer)">Martin Fowler</a>, one of the authors of the <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development#The_Agile_Manifesto" title="Agile software development">Manifesto for Agile Software Development</a>, has criticized what he calls "faux-agile" practices that are "disregarding Agile's values and principles",<sup id="cite_ref-stateofagile_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stateofagile-53"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>53<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and "the Agile Industrial Complex imposing methods upon people" contrary to the Agile principle of valuing "individuals and interactions over processes and tools"<sup id="cite_ref-agilemanifesto_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agilemanifesto-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and allowing the individuals doing the work to decide how the work is done, changing processes to suit their needs.
</p><p>In September 2016, Ron Jeffries, a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Agile_software_development" title="Agile software development">Agile Manifesto</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-agilemanifesto_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agilemanifesto-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> described what he called "Dark Scrum", saying that "Scrum can be very unsafe for programmers."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>54<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
